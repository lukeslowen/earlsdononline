Monty's Letter

The following letter was written in April 1917 by a fellow soldier, known to us only as Monty, who served alongside Bill Drury in the 2/5 Leicestershire Regiment. It was written during the pursuit of the retreating German army to the Hindenburg Line, and gives a vivid first-hand account of the conditions and experiences shared by Bill and his comrades. The letter was preserved and passed down through the family.

Saturday 21st April 1917

My dear Mother, Father, Frank and Nancy,

It is with the greatest pleasure and thankfulness that I am at last (after about 5 days) able to write a line or two again. I have tried this last week to get a letter through, but I don't think any has been sent. We have just come out of the front line after being in it 3 weeks and 4 days and I must say, I have never experienced such things before, and I hope I never shall again. Talk about hell upon earth, I think the other place must be a palace to this! At present I am quite happy, (at least as happy as one can be in France) and I assure you apart from being hungry, we are quite happy. I'm in the pink, but am very, very surprised at it.

A month ago we were in the trenches, we went back a few miles to a hut (of a kind) for about 4 days — what is called "rest". You work all day, but you do get a night's rest. After leaving this rest place we again started after the Germans, with only about three quarters of the battalion. The others were ill, hospital etc. We did a 3 day march, sleeping anywhere, before we caught the line which we were to hold. We stayed at this village; the first night we had no shelter — practically we made a bit of a cover and it rained like blazes, we only had one blanket and got nicely wet. The following day in our spare time we rigged up a sort of hut and after 3 or 4 days here, we went after the Germans again. Righteous work I can tell you.

The following day we had — that is our Regiment — to attack and capture 2 villages (Hervilly and Hesbecourt); you have to read both names in the papers, because I've seen it. We captured both, took a few prisoners, and when we had been in the village for a short time they shelled us to blazes. We stuck it and consolidated the ground, which the other regiments hadn't done. We suffered rather a lot, chiefly wounded. We were stuck in this heap of bricks and mortar about a fortnight all together, going forward to our positions from it. Of course we had no home so we had to start building. We got wood and stuff and built one. We used about three days during which of course we were shelled, and the third day a shell dropped right in the middle of our little home and up she went. This, you will admit, was cheek on the part of the Boche. I went over and told him to stop, but he wouldn't. Anyway we had got out of it just before this particular shell came so I'm alright. We had to build again.

You understand, Mother dear, that my job is to take despatches and messages from our battalion to another etc., which is not such a fine job, though I would sooner — much sooner — be on this job than any other in the line. Of course we go in the line just the same, but are not messed about quite as much. There are 4 of us on this job (another was Bill Drury), so we cover a long way in just one day.

After this village the other battalions went out for a rest. The 5th Leicesters, because they'd done well, had to stay in the line — your own battalion relieves another in the line. A day or two after this, 2 other battalions tried to take a chalk pit where the Germans were and a village (Villeret) and some rifle pits. They couldn't take them so the 5th went up. We were told if we could take these places we could have a relief. We went up and took all three places, with men absolutely done up. But I'm proud to say they did well with bomb and bayonet. They went right through the village at the very heels of the Germans. Mud was in some places knee deep without the least exaggeration, and it is this that is the trouble, not the Germans so much.

When a fellow goes up the line in the weather we have been having, he knows for a fact that he is coming back sludged to the neck and wet through. This is what puts years on us. I know that last Sunday night — don't worry about this, I'm only giving you a little idea — we were in the open, snow, rain and mud, Sunday night, all day Monday and Tuesday night, and it rained all the time and we stood in about 1 foot of slush. Of course we had no shelter, practically no sleep, only a waterproof sheet which only covers down to our hips, and we were fighting all the time. This was just an extra bit for the fighting 5th. As we had captured the other places we had to capture this one, which we did, and we were congratulated by wire by the Army Corps Command. I think we were the battalion — and I know for a fact the only one in our Brigade — because the Lincolns and other Leicesters tried and failed.

So there now, Mother dear, you have in a nutshell what your little boy's been seeing and doing in this last 3 weeks. When it is like this, letters are not taken in and I've been worrying to get a letter off all this time. Now Mother dear, don't worry if you don't hear from me at times. I shall never miss an opportunity of writing to you, as I know how eagerly you look forward to it.

Then we came back to our old village for our relief, which came after 3 weeks and 4 days. We came out of the line at 6 o'clock and had our first sleep for long enough, and at 2 o'clock we packed up again and came to the rear about 5 miles — to one of those villages we were at about a fortnight ago (Hancourt). A 5 mile march with equipment after the front lines knocks a chap over for a bit, I can tell you.

Now we'll stop talking about experiences. Oh, just a minute — when we got here expecting a home, we had a little cellar, a bit bigger than ours, for 15 of us, and it had been knocked in by a shell and was 7 feet deep in bricks and mortar which had to be cleared before we could have a night's sleep and a roof put on. Anyway, undismayed, we worked at it with a pick and shovel for 6 hours. Carrying debris up steps was hard work. We got half done and had a sleep, then finished it the next day. This is the troops' "rest". Then a message came at 11 o'clock at night which I had to take, as it was my turn out.

Well, no, Mother dear and all, I'm as happy as a little rabbit. I've had a good night's sleep, and we've had another blanket issued, which makes 2 now — we used to have 5 each in Blighty. I didn't get up till 9 — nobody knew though. I've just cleaned up, had a wash and my first shave for about a fortnight. I'd a beard like Jeremiah. Got a new pair of puttees, threw my steel hat in a corner and put my cloth one on, and I feel a new man.

Now dears, I must thank you very much indeed for that lovely parcel you sent me dated April 10th, which I received about 4 days ago. I got the parcel as I was going up the line. I ate the mince tart, which was glorious, and packed the other good things where I could. The Tommy's cooker will come in handy sometime when we are in the line, and the sweets are fine. I'm keeping those till I need them more, and the cig case was very fine indeed — just the very thing — and the Gold Flake were ripping. I've had nothing but issue cigarettes and Woodbines for a good while. The cigs issued are made of the commonest stuff possible, specially to send to the troops. I think it is a scandal, it is so. The cake was lovely but slightly smashed, as were the biscuits. The Oxo and cocoa cubes and Nestlé's milk are the things. It is grand to be able to have a drink at any time, as we can nearly always get a fire when we are not actually in the line itself. Oh, and the chocolate — that was absolutely it.

Oh swish — I've just had a bath and a clean change, the first for nearly a month. You know you sent me that Harrison Pomade? I was absolutely crummy, and I put some on 3 mornings and at present I'm quite clear of lice. It's ripping stuff.

I don't know if it's the crowd of prisoners we've got, but somebody is taking our rations. I'll just tell you what we had yesterday — not enough to feed our Teddy. Breakfast: ¼ tin of bully beef, 1/10th of a loaf. Dinner: a drop of mutton stew. Tea: 2 dry biscuits. No jam, no butter, no supper and practically no dinner — and they say that we have 5 hot meals in France! There should be only 2 men to a loaf; we had 10! But enough of this grumbling. I'm still alive and kicking and in the pink. Last night I got my bike and went 10 miles — it was a fine day — and found a canteen just setting out. I managed to get 10 little 2½d tins of ham and chicken paste. My pal and I shared, and we did have a supper, though we'd only one biscuit. Bread is the great necessity. Could you please send me a little loaf next time and a bit of butter if you can?

Well then, yesterday I went scouting again and about 3 miles away I found a canteen. I could get 2 packets of biscuits and 10 bars of choc — oh I didn't snap it up — and some for the other boys with me, 15 of us. We shall probably be paid in a day or two. We might hear the band tomorrow — they've been at the back 27 miles away while we've been amongst these little bits of iron, and they've just come up to us. It has been fine these last few days. I do hope it keeps fine; then the men in the line won't care a hang. They don't worry so much about the shells — it's being wet through.

If anyone says I don't write, please apologise. I haven't had a minute yet, but hope to this week. So cheerio won't you dear. I'm absolutely OK and happy so don't worry.

xxx Bye bye, heaps of love to all xxx
Lots of love — Your ever loving little boy — Monty xxx

Lt Borbian, my commander (a very nice boy, tell Nancy, but engaged) says I've written enough, so I must chuck it or the letter won't be in time. Bye bye.

Thank Nancy for her letter xxx — Heaps of love — Monty
