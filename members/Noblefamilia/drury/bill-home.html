Bill Drury — Home Front

1914 – 1916

(text in italics is extracted from the 2/5 Leicesters War Diary)

Bill Drury enlisted at Loughborough Drill Hall in 1915, he was just 17 years old.

His unit would form part of the 2/1st (North Midland) Division. Infantry Battalions were raised by Companies recruited in the towns and villages of Staffordshire, Lincolnshire, Leicestershire, Nottinghamshire and Derbyshire. Men were instructed in foot drill, physical training, company drill etc. The greatest difficulty was experienced in getting uniforms, and most of the recruits paraded in civilian clothes, or partly in civilian and partly in uniform, for many weeks. Throughout 1915 some members of the 2/5 Leicesters also provided guards for the prisoner of war camp at Donnington Hall.

7/1/1915 - Loughborough

The battalion was assembled in Loughborough during the January of 1915. Under their territorial officers the men were equipped and clothed and began their basic training.

29/1/1915 - The Battalion moved to Luton to form 2/1st Lincoln and Leicester Brigade.

At Luton the battalion was billeted in private houses throughout the town using schools and halls for headquarters and assembly areas. Rations were drawn from unit stores and cooked in the billets. A Divisional Headquarters was established at Luton Library (later moving to the Prudential Buildings). Transport and other materials gradually materialised and musketry was practiced at the local ranges and at Dunstable. There was a severe lack of all kinds of equipment and it was necessary to improvise and requisition from civilian sources.

30/1/1915 - Luton

5/2/1915 - Battalion designation changed to 2/5th Leicestershire Rgt, 2/1 North Midland Division

24/2/1915 - Epping. Employed in trench digging exercises

10/3/1915 - Return to Luton

11/3/1915 - Japanese arms issued.

11/4/1915 - Inspection at Stockwood Park

16/7/1915 - Under canvass at Stockwood Park

7/8/1915 - St Albans (Brigade Camp)

The Brigade assembled under canvas at St Albans at 'Briton Camp'. Training here included Brigade and Divisional manoeuvres and numerous inspections. There were also long route marches with full equipment. Pigs were purchased and fattened on swill from various cookhouses but had to be destroyed due to swine fever.

13/10/1915 - 9.10pm, During the night hostile aircraft flew over the camp. One Zeppelin type aircraft was actually seen over the camp, it approached from the south and disappeared over the west. It was flying very high. The night was dark but starlit. Later about 9.35pm another (or the same) was seen in the direction of London . Searchlights illuminated it clearly several times and shells were observed to burst near it as it moved east. Later still, about 9.50pm a series of bright flashes apparently on the ground were observed in the south east.

20/10/1915 - Harpenden . Billets.

The Brigade remained under canvas until October but as temperatures fell they were moved back to billets in Harpenden. The YMCA was a favourite off duty destination where the men could get a cup of tea and a piece of cake very cheaply. At about this time the Division was reported upon by the War Office as "organised for overseas service" - being the first of the war raised second line Territorial Divisions to be made ready. The Division was detailed as the Mobile Division for Defence of the Coast. An elaborate "Railway Entrainment Scheme" was devised and trains were placed in sidings at Watford and around Hertfordshire for swift movement in case of coastal invasion. Drills were held with the objective of moving the entire Division in less than 24 hours. Home duties had led to some members of the 59th dubbing themselves the "Lost Division" as they had lost hope of ever making it to France.

23/11/1915 - Japanese arms withdrawn, .303's issued.

13/1/1916 - New recruits have been arriving daily. GOC 3rd Army.

Throughout January parties of officers from the 59th Division were sent to France on tours of instruction in the trenches. In March, orders were received to prepare to proceed overseas.

14/3/1916 - Short rifles issued, long rifles withdrawn.

3/4/1916 - Vaccination of battalion begins.

Arrangements were made to concentrate the Division at Codford for its active service equipment and an advanced party was dispatched there. At this time the 177th Brigade was commanded by Brigadier General CG Blackadder. Throughout this period rumours circulated about the immediate embarkation for the front. However in April all plans for overseas service were suddenly cancelled at the last minute owing to the rebellion in Ireland on Easter Monday, 1916.

25/4/1916 (Easter Monday) - 1600 Harpenden. Order received to move at midnight to Liverpool

The order caught everyone by surprise. Many troops were on leave, enjoying the Easter weekend and the fair at Harpenden Common. The Brigades were rapidly gathered, the 178th and 176th Brigades moved first, followed by the 177th. Few of the officers and none of the men knew that they were headed for Ireland. On 24th April Irish Rebels had stormed the Dublin GPO and other sites across the city. The rebellion had caught the authorities off guard and an urgent telegram message to London called for reinforcements. They sailed for Kingstown on the RMS Ulster, a fast Irish mail boat diverted from Holyhead, escorted by a Royal Navy destroyer.

26/4/1916 - 7am arrived Liverpool - detrained and went by train to Crosby Camp . 19.00 orders arrived to march to Crosby and entrain for Liverpool and embark. Transport to follow later. Embarked on SS Ulster, sailed from Liverpool at midnight
